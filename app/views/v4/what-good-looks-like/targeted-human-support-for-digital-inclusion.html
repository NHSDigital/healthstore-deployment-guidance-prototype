Use targeted human support to enable digital inclusion

The Roxton Practice offers more than one way to use the same service, because one digital route will never suit everyone.

The practice learned that tablets fixed to the waiting room wall did not work: the people who needed help stayed away. What has worked has been combining technology with human-to-human support.

People can use their own phone or computer to get care and share their data. Or they can use a computer in the community, a GP surgery, a library, a pharmacy or a workplace, with someone on hand to help. People move between these routes as their needs change.

The practice puts trained helpers and volunteer "care connectors" where people already go. They can help patients set up the NHS App and use it to track a condition and send in readings.

The practice has found that employing non-clinical staff to help assist people with technology in the waiting areas has paid off for them financially, and is now considering how it reorganises its lobby areas to offer more rooms for private one-to-one digital support from non-clinical staff.
