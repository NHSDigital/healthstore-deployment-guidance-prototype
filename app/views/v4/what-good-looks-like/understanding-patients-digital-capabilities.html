Find ways of understanding patients' digital capabilities

Before suggesting any digital tool, the Roxton Practice works out whether each patient can actually use one and has built this into the information they gather about their patients.

The practice asked all 12,500 of its patients about their confidence and their preferences for digital care. It did this in several ways: face-to-face, by phone, by letter, by text and online, so it did not miss the people least likely to reply online.

Each patient is then given a digital readiness level, kept in their health record. Staff use it to decide how to reach that person, and sometimes communications preferences are coded into locally-designed pathways. Someone confident might get a link or a text sent to them. Someone less confident is offered a printed form, or face-to-face help.

This information is built into the electronic patient record. This means clinicians and practice staff are prompted to keep it up to date.
